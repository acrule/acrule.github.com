Working with Adam and Jim

Let me start by saying, Welcome, we're gald to have you on board. I want you to thrive during your time with us. To that end, I've put together a list of tactics to help you do first-rate research from Day 1.

Day 1

  1. Set up your space. If you're working with us more than half-time, we'll provide you with a dedicated desk in the Design Lab. We can also provide a monitor, mouse, and keyboard for your time in the group. For those working fewer hours, the Design Lab has hoteling desks and monitors you can use.
  2. Read this document. It should take < 15 minutes. Also read Jim's advice to grad students and skim Scott Klemmer's lab policies and advice to grad students. That should take you 1-2 hours. Scott is not a part of our group (though he co-directs the Design Lab) and I don't agree with all of his policies, but he has great advice on the tactics of doing good research (and his thoughts inspired this document).
  3. If you will be running an experiment with people, take the UCSD human subjects course and email me your completion certificate.
  4. Set an intention at the beginning of your rotation, write it down, and share it with me. What will you accomplish? Make a conservative goal that you're reasonably certain you can accomplish, and a stretch goal that might happen if things go really well. Having a clear intention will enable you to focus your efforts, work effectivey, and know when you're done. Decide what your final deliverable will be (working code, a conference publication, ...) and work backwards from there to put together a schedule of what you'll accomplish this quarter. Your plans will likely bounce around a bunch for the first week-and-a-half but you should have a pretty clear goal by the middle of your second week. Update your conservative and stretch goals and schedule each week. A good way to summarize all of this is to create an Individualized Development Plan (IDP). Here are some good examples.

Meetings

Our group meets every (TBD for Fall 2017) in the Design Lab. Be on time as we often video conference with collaborators in France. These group meetings are a great place to share in-progress work, get high-level feedback, and plan for the next week. Come prepared to talk for ~5 minutes about what you have accomplished since the last meeting, what you plan to do this next week, and any road-blocks your are encountering. If you want feedback on something that people will need to spend time reading or reviewing, don't expect them to read or review during the meeting. Create a handout and email it to the group at least a day before the meeting.

The student members of our group at UC San Diego have an additional meeting every week at (TBD for Fall 2017) in the Design Lab. These student meetings are a great venue for discussing the details of writing code, running studies, and analyzing data. Group meetings are for updates and strategizing. Student meetings are for tactics and getting work done.

You should attend both meetings every week. At the end of each meeting clearly state what you will accomplish by the next meeting.

The Design Lab

Our research group is part of the larger Design Lab. Being a member of the our group also means you are a part of the Design Lab. You'll get a feel for the Design Lab culture over time, but here are some things you should know and do up front:

  1. Ask me to add you to design-students-l@mailman.ucsd.edu if you have not already been added to the Design Lab students mailing list. This goes out to all the undergrads, MS, PhD, and Post-docs in the lab and gets you on all the other lab mailing lists.
  2. Lab meeting is every week at 2p on Wednesdays. Feel free to come and hear about ongoing work in the lab from other profs, post-docs, and students.
  3. Every quarter we have a Design@Large seminar to hear from some of the leading researchers and practicioners in HCI. For Fall 2017, we meet every Wednesday from 4-5.15p, usually in CSE 1202
  4. Lab events like Design@Large are on the Design Lab Google calendar. If you don't have access, email me.
  5. Go to the Locals Google Drive folder for any lab related question. Again, email me if you don't have access.
  6. All the whiteboards are fair game. If you need to erase something to make space, take a picture of it first.
  7. Help yourself to snacks and coffee in the kitchen. Just clean up after yourself.

Reading

Great ideas rarely come out of the blue, so make time to regularly read relevant (and irrelevant) literature. If you want to do research in the future, learn the art of reading research papers. Expect to read about 25 papers to get up to speed on a topic (e.g. automatic tutorial generation from screen recordings). If you find a useful paper or one that the group should know about, upload a pdf to the appropriate folder in the papers folder of our Google Drive and add a 2-4 sentence summary of the paper to the related work summaries. Include links to the paper and any research videos. Research videos are a great way to quickly share cool research. If the paper is really good, email a link to the paper, video, or both to the group.

Writing

Clear writing begets clear thinking. Make a habit of writing about your work, both what you have accomplished and what you plan to do. Start writing your papers well in advance of the deadline, and write each section as soon as reasonably possible. For example, you should write your methods section as you are running the study.

Grad School Advice

  1. When defining and evaluating your research, use the Heilmeier Catechism
  2. Read Getting What you Came For and PhD Grind
  3. Set boundaries on your work life so your personal and work life can thrive. You'll do much better work if you are well fed, well rested, and well loved so make time to eat right, exercise, sleep, and spend time with people who matter to you. For some great advice on doing deep work in grad school while limiting your time in the office see Cal Newport's blog.

Projects

All of our research revoles around helping people track, share, and make sense of the process of their work. Ask me if you want to see the NSF grant proposal(s) that spawned this line of work. Under that umbrella, we have a number of research efforts:

Current

  • Observing Jupyter Notebook Use - How is it that data analysts track their work in Jupyter Notebooks, and how can we build extensions to those notebooks that make it easier for them to conduct reporducable, easily shared analyses?
  • Enhancing Jupyter Notebook for Education - How might we develop a data analysis curriculum and enhance Jupyter Notebooks to help students learn to conduct high-quality reproducatble analyses?
  • Synopsis - Helping clinicians find the most relevant information in a large collection of patient notes by highlighting what other clinicians have paid attention to in the past.

Past

  • Traces - Traces is our main activity recorder for tracking clicks, keystrokes, apps used, window events, and taking periodic screenshots on Mac computers.
  • Resumption Methods - Adam's 2nd year project for Cog Sci qualification looked at what people need to know to resume computer-mediated work and how they use distributed cognition to reconstruct that information.
  • Visualization - Once we've recorded work activity, we need to visualize it in useful ways that leverage people's cognitive strengths to reconstruct the details of past activities. Early work on using small thumbnails to represent past activity was published at Cog Sci 2015.
  • Privacy - Detailed tracking of work activity is very invasive. Karen Boyd leads our efforts to understand the privacy concerns people have with tracking work-related activity. Here is a preliminary writeup of this work.
